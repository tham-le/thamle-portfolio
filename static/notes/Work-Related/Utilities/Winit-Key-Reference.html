WTF Are My winit Key Repeats Disappearing?

”The OS is sending you the repeats. Winit is giving them to you. The bug is in the lock, and it’s on your side of the door.”

Okay, this changes everything—in a good way. You’re using winit. This means you’ve already followed “Path 1” from our last discussion. You’re listening to the high-level, translated event stream. The telegraph operator (the OS) is doing its job and dutifully sending the repeat KeyPress events, and winit is dutifully handing them to your application.

So if they’re arriving at your doorstep, why aren’t they making it to the Windows host?

The answer is almost certainly because your event-handling logic is, with the best of intentions, filtering them out. You’ve written code that effectively says, “I already know this key is down, so I don’t need to do anything with this new ‘down’ event.”

The Analogy: The Over-Efficient Bouncer

Imagine your application is a nightclub, and keyboard events are guests trying to get in. You’ve hired a bouncer (your event loop logic) to manage the door.

  • Initial Press: The ‘A’ key arrives for the first time. The bouncer checks his list, sees ‘A’ is not inside, and says, “Yep, new guest. Go on in.” He adds ‘A’ to his list of current patrons.
  • The Repeat: A millisecond later, another ‘A’ key event arrives. This is the repeat event from the OS. The bouncer looks at the event, looks at his list, and says, “Whoa, hold on. My list says ‘A’ is already inside. This must be a duplicate. Denied.”
  • The Release: Finally, a KeyRelease event for ‘A’ arrives. The bouncer says, “Okay, ‘A’ is leaving.” He removes ‘A’ from his list.

Your bouncer is too smart for his own good! He’s trying to maintain a “state” of which keys are in the club, but in doing so, he’s throwing out the very repeat events you need.

The winit Event Model: Repeats are Just More Pressed Events

This is the crucial piece of technical information: winit does not have a special Event::KeyboardRepeat. It reports a key being held down by sending you multiple Event::WindowEvent::KeyboardInput events, and for each one, the state will be ElementState::Pressed.

The only way to distinguish the first press from a repeat is to track the state yourself. But for your use case—forwarding everything—you don’t need to distinguish them at all! You want to treat every single Pressed event as a new command to be sent over the wire.

The Big Fix

The solution is to remove the state-tracking logic (HashSet, etc.) from your client’s event loop. You must process and forward an event for every single KeyboardInput you receive from winit, regardless of whether you’ve seen that key before. Each ElementState::Pressed event, whether it’s the first or the fiftieth, maps directly to one call to your inject_key function on the host.
