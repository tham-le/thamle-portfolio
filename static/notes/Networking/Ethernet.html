Ethernet

It’s the reason your computer can talk to your router without screaming into the void

The Office Memo Analogy

Imagine an office where everyone sits in cubicles. You need to send a memo to Dave in accounting. You can’t just yell across the office (well, you could, but HR would have words). Instead, you write “TO: DAVE” on an envelope, put your memo inside, and drop it on the shared mail cart.

Everyone in the office has access to the mail cart. The mail clerk picks up your envelope, sees it’s for Dave, walks over to Dave’s cubicle, and drops it on his desk. Dave opens it, reads your memo, done.

That’s Ethernet. It’s Layer 2 of the networking stack, the Data Link Layer. Its job is simple: deliver frames between devices on the same local network using physical addresses.

Why Do We Need Ethernet?

The Network-Layer gives us IP addresses and routing. That handles the big picture: “get this packet from New York to Tokyo.”

But at some point, your packet needs to actually move across a wire (or through the air) from one physical device to the next physical device. Your computer to your router. Your router to the ISP’s router. The server to its router.

Ethernet solves this local delivery problem. It says: “On this particular wire, how do we identify devices and send data between them?”

Without Ethernet (or something like it), the Network Layer would be useless. You’d have IP addresses but no way to actually transmit the bits.

The Broadcast Medium Problem

Here’s the original problem Ethernet solved back in the 1970s.

Early networks used a single shared cable. Imagine a long coaxial cable running through an office. Every computer taps into this cable. When one computer sends data, everyone hears it.

This creates chaos:

  • Collision: What if two computers transmit at the same time? Their signals interfere, garbage.
  • Addressing: How does a computer know if a message is for them or someone else?
  • Fairness: How do we prevent one chatty computer from hogging the whole cable?

Ethernet solved all three problems.

MAC Addresses: Your Network Card’s Name

Every network interface has a MAC address (Media Access Control address). It’s a 48 bit unique identifier burned into the hardware at the factory.

Example: 00:1A:2B:3C:4D:5E

The first 24 bits identify the manufacturer (like 00:1A:2B might be Cisco). The last 24 bits are the specific device number.

MAC addresses are flat. Unlike IP addresses, they don’t have a network portion and a host portion. They’re just unique identifiers, like a serial number.

This is critical: MAC addresses identify physical hardware, while IP addresses identify logical network locations. Your laptop keeps the same MAC address whether you’re at home, at a coffee shop, or on Mars. But your IP address changes based on which network you’re on.

The Ethernet Frame Structure

Ethernet wraps data in frames. Here’s what a frame looks like:

Let me break down each part:

Destination MAC (6 bytes)

Who is this frame for? Every device on the local network looks at this field. If it matches their MAC address (or is a broadcast address), they process it. Otherwise, they ignore it.

Source MAC (6 bytes)

Who sent this? So the receiver knows where to send the reply.

EtherType (2 bytes)

What kind of data is in the payload? Common values:

  • 0x0800: IPv4 packet inside
  • 0x0806: ARP request/reply inside
  • 0x86DD: IPv6 packet inside

This tells the receiver which protocol to pass the payload to after stripping off the Ethernet header.

Payload (46 to 1500 bytes)

The actual data. Usually an IP packet. Minimum size is 46 bytes (padded if necessary). Maximum is 1500 bytes, called the MTU (Maximum Transmission Unit).

Why 1500? Historical reasons. When Ethernet was designed, memory was expensive. 1500 bytes was a good compromise between efficiency and buffer size. We’re stuck with it now for compatibility.

Frame Check Sequence (4 bytes)

A CRC checksum calculated over the entire frame. The receiver recalculates it. If it doesn’t match, the frame was corrupted in transit and gets dropped silently.

Notice what’s NOT in the Ethernet header: no sequence numbers, no acknowledgments, no retransmission. Ethernet is unreliable. If a frame gets corrupted or lost, Ethernet doesn’t care. That’s TCP’s job at a higher layer.

How Ethernet Sends Data

Let’s trace what happens when your computer sends data to your router.

Step 1: Your Computer Builds a Frame

Your computer wants to send an IP packet to 8.8.8.8 (Google DNS). The Network-Layer determines: “This isn’t on my local network, send it to the gateway at 192.168.1.1.”

But Ethernet needs a MAC address, not an IP address. So your computer thinks: “I know the router’s IP (192.168.1.1), but what’s its MAC address?”

Step 2: ARP (Address Resolution Protocol)

Your computer broadcasts an ARP request on the local network:

Ethernet frame:
  dst_mac: FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF (broadcast, everyone hears this)
  src_mac: 00:11:22:33:44:55 (your computer)
  ethertype: 0x0806 (ARP)
  payload: "Who has 192.168.1.1? Tell 00:11:22:33:44:55"

Every device on the network receives this. Your router recognizes its IP address and sends back an ARP reply:

Ethernet frame:
  dst_mac: 00:11:22:33:44:55 (your computer)
  src_mac: AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF (router)
  ethertype: 0x0806 (ARP)
  payload: "192.168.1.1 is at AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF"

Your computer caches this in its ARP table so it doesn’t have to ask again for a few minutes.

Output might look like:

? (192.168.1.105) at 11:22:33:44:55:66 on en0 [ethernet]

Step 3: Send the Actual Data

Now your computer knows the router’s MAC address. It builds the real frame:

Ethernet frame:
  dst_mac: AA:BB:CC:DD:EE:FF (router)
  src_mac: 00:11:22:33:44:55 (your computer)
  ethertype: 0x0800 (IPv4)
  payload: [IP packet with destination 8.8.8.8]
  fcs: [checksum]

Your network card converts this into electrical signals (or radio waves for WiFi) and transmits it.

Step 4: The Router Receives It

The router’s network card receives the signals, converts them back to bits, and checks the destination MAC. It matches! The router processes the frame:

  1. Verify the FCS checksum (frame intact?)
  2. Strip off the Ethernet header
  3. Look at the ethertype (0x0800 = IPv4)
  4. Pass the IP packet up to the Network-Layer
  5. The Network Layer routes it to the next hop

The router then wraps the IP packet in a new Ethernet frame for the next hop. Different destination MAC, different source MAC, but the IP packet inside is untouched.

CSMA/CD: Avoiding Collisions (The Old Way)

In the early days with shared cables, Ethernet used CSMA/CD (Carrier Sense Multiple Access with Collision Detection). It’s like a polite conversation:

Carrier Sense: Before talking, listen. Is someone else already transmitting? If yes, wait.

Multiple Access: Everyone can transmit, but only one at a time.

Collision Detection: If two devices start transmitting at exactly the same time, they detect the collision (garbage on the wire) and both stop immediately.

When a collision happens:

  1. Both devices stop transmitting
  2. They send a “jam signal” to make sure everyone knows there was a collision
  3. Each waits a random amount of time (randomness prevents them from colliding again)
  4. Try again

This worked, but it was inefficient. The more devices on the network, the more collisions, the slower everything got.

Switches: The Modern Solution

Modern Ethernet uses switches, and switches changed everything.

A switch is a smart device that learns which MAC addresses are connected to which ports. When a frame comes in, the switch looks at the destination MAC and forwards it only to the port where that device lives.

Here’s how it works:

Learning Phase

When the switch first powers on, it knows nothing. Its MAC address table is empty.

  1. Device A (MAC AA:AA:AA:AA:AA:AA) sends a frame to Device B
  2. Switch receives the frame on port 1
  3. Switch thinks: “Aha! MAC AA:AA:AA:AA:AA:AA is on port 1” and adds it to the table
  4. Switch doesn’t know where Device B is yet, so it floods the frame to all ports except port 1
  5. Device B receives it and sends a reply
  6. Switch sees the reply from Device B (MAC BB:BB:BB:BB:BB:BB) on port 3
  7. Switch thinks: “Aha! MAC BB:BB:BB:BB:BB:BB is on port 3” and adds it to the table

Now the switch knows:

Port 1: AA:AA:AA:AA:AA:AA
Port 3: BB:BB:BB:BB:BB:BB

Forwarding Phase

Next time Device A sends to Device B:

  1. Switch receives frame on port 1
  2. Looks at destination MAC: BB:BB:BB:BB:BB:BB
  3. Checks table: “Port 3!”
  4. Forwards frame only to port 3

Device C on port 2 never sees this frame. No more shared medium, no more collisions, everyone can transmit simultaneously.

This is why modern Ethernet is full duplex: you can send and receive at the same time. Old Ethernet was half duplex: only send or receive, not both.

Broadcast and Multicast

Sometimes you want to talk to everyone. Ethernet supports this:

Broadcast MAC: FF:FF:FF:FF:FF:FF Frames with this destination go to every device on the network. ARP uses this.

Multicast MAC: 01:00:5E:xx:xx:xx Frames go to a group of interested devices. Used for things like video streaming to multiple receivers.

Switches forward broadcast and multicast frames to all ports (except the incoming port). This can cause problems on large networks, which is why we segment networks with routers or VLANs.

VLANs: Virtual Networks

A VLAN (Virtual Local Area Network) lets you split one physical switch into multiple logical networks.

Imagine an office with one switch, but you want the sales team isolated from the engineering team. You configure:

  • Ports 1-8: VLAN 10 (sales)
  • Ports 9-16: VLAN 20 (engineering)

Devices in VLAN 10 can’t see frames from VLAN 20, even though they’re on the same physical switch. It’s like having two separate switches.

The naming scheme: <speed>BASE-<medium>

  • Speed in Mbps (10, 100, 1000, etc.)
  • BASE means baseband (the whole cable is used for one signal)
  • Medium: T for twisted pair copper, SR/LR for fiber

Modern networks typically use Gigabit Ethernet (1 Gbps) for desktops and 10 Gbps or higher for servers and uplinks.

WiFi: Ethernet’s Wireless Cousin

WiFi is technically not Ethernet. It’s a different standard (IEEE 802.11 vs IEEE 802.3). But at the Data Link Layer, it does the same job: deliver frames using MAC addresses.

The big differences:

  • Shared medium: WiFi is inherently broadcast. Everyone in range hears everything.
  • CSMA/CA: Uses Collision Avoidance instead of Detection (you can’t detect collisions while transmitting on radio)
  • ACKs: WiFi is more reliable than wired Ethernet. It requires acknowledgments for every frame.
  • More overhead: WiFi frames have extra fields for managing the wireless medium

But from the Network-Layer’s perspective, WiFi looks just like Ethernet. Same MAC addresses, same frame structure (mostly), same ARP protocol.

Common Issues and Debugging

Duplicate IP Addresses

If two devices on the same network have the same IP address, weird shit happens. Their ARP entries conflict, and packets go to the wrong device.

ARP Poisoning

An attacker sends fake ARP replies: “Hey, I’m the router! Send all your traffic to me!” This is a classic man-in-the-middle attack. Your frames go to the attacker instead of the real router.

Defense: Use static ARP entries for critical devices, or deploy ARP spoofing detection.

Broadcast Storms

If you connect switches in a loop without Spanning Tree Protocol (STP), broadcast frames circle forever, multiplying exponentially, and the network melts down in seconds.

STP detects loops and disables redundant links. It’s boring but critical.

MTU Mismatches

If your computer thinks the MTU is 1500 bytes, but a router along the path only supports 1400 bytes, packets get fragmented or dropped.

The Big Picture

Ethernet is the workhorse of local networking. It:

  • Identifies devices using MAC addresses
  • Delivers frames on the local network
  • Resolves addresses using ARP
  • Handles collisions (or avoids them with switches)

It’s simple, it’s reliable, it’s everywhere. Your home network uses it. Data centers use it (at much higher speeds). It’s the foundation that everything else builds on.

Ethernet's Scope Ethernet only works on a single network segment. Once a packet needs to leave your local network, Ethernet hands it off to a Router, which strips off the Ethernet frame and routes the IP packet to the next hop. The next hop wraps it in a new Ethernet frame for its local delivery.

Think of Ethernet like the postal carrier who delivers mail within your neighborhood. They don’t care about the national postal system. They just read the address on the envelope and drop it at the right house.
