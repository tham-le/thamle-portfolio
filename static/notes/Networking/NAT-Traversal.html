WTF is NAT Traversal?

Two machines, no public IP, somehow talking directly. Here’s the trick.

You set up WireGuard between your laptop at home and a server at a friend’s house. Neither of you has a static public IP. Neither of you has opened any ports. And yet ping 10.0.0.2 works and the latency is 12ms, which means packets are going directly between your machines, not through some relay in a datacenter.

How does that happen?

This is NAT traversal. It’s the core problem that Tailscale, NetBird, and every peer-to-peer system you’ve ever used has had to solve. Once you understand the mechanism, you’ll understand exactly why Tailscale’s DERP servers exist, when they’re used, and why the direct path works the vast majority of the time.

This article builds on TUN-TAP. We already know that WireGuard reads and writes raw IP packets through a TUN device. This article answers: once you have those packets, how do you get them across the internet to a peer you can’t directly address?

The Core Analogy

Imagine two people, Alice and Bob, working in separate offices. Each office has one shared receptionist at the front desk. You can’t call Alice or Bob directly because they don’t have their own phone lines. Every call goes through the receptionist.

Now Alice and Bob want a private conversation, bypassing the receptionist entirely. Here’s the trick: Alice calls Bob’s receptionist and says “I’m at extension 5001, please tell Bob.” Bob calls Alice’s receptionist and says “I’m at extension 6002, please tell Alice.” Now Alice calls Bob directly at 6002, and Bob calls Alice at 5001. Both receptionists let the calls through, because each side placed an outbound call first and the receptionist recognizes the number.

That’s UDP hole punching. The NAT device (the “receptionist”) only blocks unsolicited incoming traffic. If you first make an outbound connection, the NAT opens a temporary hole that lets return traffic flow in. The trick is getting both sides to punch their holes at the same time, coordinated by a third party.

The Problem: NAT Is a One-Way Door

IP addresses are 32 bits, giving roughly 4.3 billion unique values. There are far more than 4.3 billion internet-connected devices today. We ran out of IPv4 addresses around 2011. The solution keeping the internet running since then is NAT (Network Address Translation).

Your home router has one public IP. Every device on your home network gets a private address (10.x.x.x, 192.168.x.x, or 172.16.x.x, all reserved and non-routable on the public internet). When you send a packet out, your router rewrites the source IP from your private address to its public one, and maintains a translation table so it knows how to route replies back in.

Your laptop: 192.168.1.5:54321  ->  Router: 203.0.113.1:8765  ->  Server: 1.2.3.4:443

The router’s NAT table entry for this connection:

Private              Public               Remote           Protocol
192.168.1.5:54321    203.0.113.1:8765     1.2.3.4:443      UDP

When the server replies to 203.0.113.1:8765, the router looks up the table and forwards the packet inward to 192.168.1.5:54321. This works perfectly for client-initiated traffic.

The problem: what if a remote machine tries to reach your laptop without you initiating the connection first? There’s no entry in the NAT table. The router sees an incoming packet addressed to 203.0.113.1:8765 with no matching entry and drops it silently. Your laptop is completely unreachable from outside.

Two devices, both behind NAT, cannot connect to each other directly. There’s no public address for either one. This is the fundamental problem NAT traversal solves.

Step 1: Discovering Your Own Public Address via STUN

Before you can tell a peer how to reach you, you need to know how the outside world sees you. Your laptop knows it’s at 192.168.1.5:51820, but the internet sees it through your router as something like 203.0.113.1:8765. You don’t know that mapping unless you ask someone on the public internet.

STUN (Session Traversal Utilities for NAT, RFC 5389) answers this question.

A STUN server is a simple public-internet service. You send it a UDP packet, it replies with the source IP and port it observed on your incoming packet. That observed address is your NAT’s current public mapping for that connection.

Your Laptop                       STUN Server (public internet)
192.168.1.5:51820                 stun.example.com:3478

   -- "What's my public address?" ---------------------->

   <--------------------- "You look like 203.0.113.1:8765"

One UDP packet, one reply, you know your public endpoint. STUN is genuinely that simple at its core.

The STUN response carries a field called XOR-MAPPED-ADDRESS. The IP and port are XOR-encoded against a magic cookie value (0x2112A442, defined in the RFC). Some home routers “helpfully” rewrite IP addresses they find in UDP payloads (the ALG feature). XOR-encoding the address means the value doesn’t look like a raw IP address to those routers, so they leave it alone.

Step 2: The NAT Taxonomy (This Determines If Punching Works)

Not all NATs behave the same. Whether hole punching succeeds depends entirely on your NAT’s mapping behavior. RFC 4787 gives us the modern classification.

Endpoint-Independent Mapping (Easy NAT)

The NAT assigns the same external port to all outbound packets from your 192.168.1.5:51820, regardless of destination. If STUN tells you you’re at 203.0.113.1:8765, you’ll still be at port 8765 when you send to your peer. The mapping is stable per local socket.

Most home routers do this. It means the endpoint you learned from STUN is exactly what you give to your peer. Hole punching works reliably here.

Endpoint-Dependent Mapping (Symmetric NAT, “Hard NAT”)

The NAT assigns a different external port for every distinct destination. You send to STUN server X and get port 8765. You send to peer Y and get a different port, say 8834. Your peer knows about port 8765 from STUN, sends to it, and your NAT drops the packet because there’s no entry for that source at port 8765.

Corporate firewalls and carrier-grade NAT (CGNAT) typically do this. Standard hole punching fails here.

NAT TypeMappingHole Punch?
Full ConeEndpoint-IndependentYes
Restricted ConeEndpoint-IndependentYes (must send first)
Port-Restricted ConeEndpoint-IndependentYes (must send first)
SymmetricEndpoint-DependentNo (without tricks)

Hard NAT Is Common in Production

AWS NAT Gateways, GCP Cloud NAT, Azure NAT, and most enterprise firewalls use Endpoint-Dependent Mapping. Two EC2 instances behind their respective NAT gateways will almost certainly fail to hole-punch. Tailscale will report “hard NAT” and fall back to DERP relay.

Step 3: The Hole Punch

Both Alice and Bob are behind easy NATs. They’ve both queried STUN and know their public endpoints. A coordination server has shared those endpoints between them (more on that in the next article). Now they need to actually punch.

The problem: if Alice sends a packet to Bob’s public endpoint 203.0.113.99:9001 right now, Bob’s NAT drops it. There’s no entry for Alice in Bob’s NAT table.

The solution: both sides send simultaneously.

Timeline ----------------------------------------------------------------->

[T=0ms]  Coordination server tells both: "punch NOW"

[T=1ms]  Alice sends probe -> Bob's public endpoint
         Bob's NAT: "no entry for Alice" -> DROPPED
         Alice's NAT: creates entry  192.168.1.5:51820 <-> Bob's endpoint

[T=1ms]  Bob sends probe -> Alice's public endpoint
         Alice's NAT: "no entry for Bob" -> DROPPED
         Bob's NAT: creates entry  10.0.0.5:51820 <-> Alice's endpoint

[T=2ms]  Alice sends second probe -> Bob's endpoint
         Bob's NAT: "I have an entry for Alice's endpoint" -> PASSES

[T=2ms]  Bob sends second probe -> Alice's endpoint
         Alice's NAT: "I have an entry for Bob's endpoint" -> PASSES

[T=3ms]  Bidirectional UDP channel open. WireGuard handshake proceeds.

The key insight: the first probes get dropped by the remote NAT, but they create entries in the local NAT. By the time the second probes arrive, both NATs have matching outbound entries. The holes exist on both sides.

When Punching Fails: The Relay

Sometimes the network is hostile:

  • Both peers behind hard (Endpoint-Dependent) NAT
  • A corporate firewall that blocks all inbound UDP regardless
  • Mobile CGNAT: two layers of NAT stacked, the outer one being a carrier’s shared address space
  • Timing failure where both initial probes expire before either hole is punched

In these cases you need a relay: a machine on the public internet that both peers can reach, which forwards encrypted packets between them.

The standard protocol for this in WebRTC is TURN (Traversal Using Relays around NAT, RFC 5766). It works, but it’s a stateful, complex protocol that requires authentication, allocation requests, and refresh messages. Most VPN systems don’t want to depend on it.

Tailscale built their own relay protocol: DERP (Designated Encrypted Relay for Packets). Two properties make it a better fit here:

It runs over HTTPS on port 443. Most restrictive corporate firewalls allow port 443 outbound. Standard UDP and non-standard TCP ports are often blocked. By tunneling over HTTPS, DERP reaches through even aggressive firewall policies.

It’s keyed on WireGuard public keys. The DERP server routes packets by destination public key, not by IP:port allocation. There’s no allocation step. You connect, identify yourself by public key, and start sending packets addressed to other keys. The server blindly forwards ciphertext it can’t read.

Alice                        DERP Server                        Bob
  |                               |                               |
  |-- HTTPS connect ------------->|                               |
  |                               |<------------- HTTPS connect --|
  |                               |                               |
  |-- WireGuard handshake ------->|------------ forwarded to Bob->|
  |                               |                               |
  |<-- WireGuard handshake reply -|<-------------- Bob replies ---|
  |                               |                               |
  |   [hole punch attempt runs in background]                     |
  |                               |                               |
  |-- WireGuard data (direct UDP) -------------------------------->|
      (DERP steps aside, direct path established)

DERP Cannot Decrypt Your Traffic

WireGuard encryption happens on your local device before any packet touches DERP. The relay forwards ciphertext addressed to a public key. It has no session keys. Even Tailscale’s own DERP servers are completely blind to your traffic content.

Tailscale’s Full Connection Lifecycle

Every Tailscale connection starts relayed and upgrades to direct when possible:

  1. Both devices connect to their nearest DERP server.
  2. DERP relays the initial exchange of endpoints and WireGuard public keys between the two peers.
  3. Both devices fire simultaneous hole-punch probes toward each other’s STUN-discovered endpoints.
  4. If punching succeeds, traffic migrates to direct UDP. DERP goes quiet.
  5. If punching fails, traffic stays on DERP.
  6. Tailscale retries direct connectivity in the background. A connection can silently upgrade from relayed to direct without dropping sessions if network conditions change.

According to Tailscale’s engineering blog, this machinery succeeds in establishing direct P2P connections in more than 90% of typical network conditions. DERP is genuinely a last resort in normal usage, not the default path.

Common Misconceptions

❌ “A relay means Tailscale can see my traffic.”

The relay forwards ciphertext. WireGuard session keys never leave your device. The relay is a sealed envelope carrier.

❌ “STUN is NAT traversal.”

STUN is one tool in the traversal stack. It discovers your public endpoint. It does nothing to open holes or establish bidirectional connectivity. NAT traversal is the combination of STUN for discovery, the simultaneous punch for opening the channel, and relay fallback when punching fails.

❌ “UDP hole punching is unreliable.”

For Endpoint-Independent NATs (most home routers), it’s highly reliable. Failure cases are specific: Endpoint-Dependent NATs, stacked CGNAT layers, and firewalls that aggressively expire UDP state. Tailscale reports real-world direct connection success above 90%.

❌ “Just forward a port and you don’t need any of this.”

True, but only if one peer has a static public IP and you control the router. Hole punching solves the general case: full mesh connectivity where no node has a guaranteed reachable public address.

You now know how two peers behind NAT discover each other and establish a direct channel. But we’ve skipped past the coordination step: how do Alice and Bob exchange their STUN-discovered endpoints and WireGuard public keys in the first place? That’s a separate problem entirely and it’s what separates raw WireGuard from a full mesh VPN system.
