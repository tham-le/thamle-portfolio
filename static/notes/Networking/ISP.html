WTF is an ISP?

An ISP (Internet Service Provider) is a company that provides you with internet access. They connect your home/business network to the global internet.

Think of them as the on-ramp to the internet highway.

The Highway Analogy

The Highway System

Your Home

Your private driveway (local network: 192.168.1.0/24)

ISP

The on-ramp that connects you to the highway system:

  • Provides you a public address (IP address)
  • Routes your traffic to the right destination
  • Manages the “roads” (fiber optic cables, cell towers)

Internet

The global highway system where everyone can travel

Without an ISP: You have a driveway but no way to reach the highway (no internet).

That’s an ISP - the bridge between your network and the global internet.

What an ISP Provides

1. Internet Connectivity

Your Device → Your Router → ISP Network → Internet

Physical connection: Cable, DSL, Fiber, Satellite, or Cellular.

2. Public IP Address

Private IP: 192.168.1.10  (your device)
    ↓
NAT at router
    ↓
Public IP: 203.0.113.5    (assigned by ISP)
    ↓
Internet sees this IP

Your ISP assigns the public IP that websites see.

3. DNS Servers

You: "What's the IP of google.com?"
ISP DNS: "It's 142.250.185.46"

Most people use their ISP’s DNS (though you can use others like 8.8.8.8).

4. Routing

Your packet → ISP Router → Peering point → Other ISPs → Destination

ISPs route your Packets to the rest of the internet.

5. Email (Sometimes)

example@yourISP.com

Legacy service - many ISPs provide email addresses.

ISP Tiers

Tier 1 ISPs (The Backbone)

Examples: AT&T, Verizon, Level 3, NTT

Characteristics:

  • Own global fiber optic networks
  • Peer with each other (no payment)
  • Can reach entire internet without paying upstream
  • Handle massive traffic (terabits/second)
Tier 1 ISP A ↔ Peering ↔ Tier 1 ISP B
    (free exchange)

Tier 2 ISPs (Regional)

Examples: Comcast, Charter, Cox

Characteristics:

  • Buy transit from Tier 1 ISPs
  • Peer with some ISPs
  • Serve large regions or countries
  • Handle gigabits to terabits/second
Tier 2 ISP → Pays Tier 1 for internet access
            ↔ Free peering with other Tier 2s

Tier 3 ISPs (Local)

Examples: Local cable companies, small ISPs

Characteristics:

  • Buy all transit from Tier 2 ISPs
  • Serve cities or small regions
  • Minimal or no peering
Tier 3 ISP → Pays Tier 2 → Tier 2 pays Tier 1 → Internet

How They Connect

Your ISP (Tier 3)
    ↓ (pays for transit)
Regional ISP (Tier 2)
    ↓ (pays for transit)
Global ISP (Tier 1)
    ↔ (peers for free)
Other Tier 1 ISPs
    ↓
Entire Internet

DSL (Digital Subscriber Line)

Your house → Phone line → DSLAM → ISP network

Uses: Existing copper phone lines Speed: Degrades with distance from ISP

Cable Internet

Your house → Coax cable → CMTS → ISP network

Uses: TV cable infrastructure (shared with neighbors) Speed: Can slow down during peak hours (shared bandwidth)

Fiber (FTTH - Fiber To The Home)

Your house → Fiber optic cable → OLT → ISP network

Uses: Dedicated fiber optic connection Speed: Fastest, most reliable

What ISPs See

Everything Unencrypted

HTTP Request (no [[TLS]]):
GET /search?q=secret HTTP/1.1
Host: example.com

ISP can see: URL, query parameters, all content

Encrypted Traffic (TLS)

HTTPS Request:
ClientHello → (encrypted) → Server

ISP can see:
- Destination IP (example.com's IP)
- Connection time and duration
- Amount of data transferred
- DNS queries (unless using encrypted DNS)

ISP CANNOT see:
- URL path (/search)
- Query parameters (?q=secret)
- Page content

Always use HTTPS to prevent ISP snooping.

ISP Responsibilities

1. Traffic Routing

Forward your Packets to the right destination.

2. Bandwidth Management

Your plan: 100 Mbps down / 10 Mbps up

ISP enforces these limits

3. Peering Agreements

Connect with other ISPs to exchange traffic.

4. DNS Resolution

Provide DNS servers (or let you use alternatives).

5. Customer Support

Help with connection issues (when it works…).

ISP Pricing Models

Residential

Basic:     50 Mbps  → $50/month
Standard: 200 Mbps  → $80/month
Premium: 1000 Mbps  → $120/month

Usually: Asymmetric (download > upload)

Business

Symmetric: 100/100 Mbps → $200/month
           + Static IP addresses
           + Better SLA (uptime guarantee)

Data Caps

Plan: 1000 Mbps with 1 TB data cap
Exceed: $10 per 50 GB extra

Common in US, less common elsewhere.

Peering and Transit

Peering (Free Exchange)

ISP A ↔ ISP B

"I'll send you traffic destined for your customers,
 you send me traffic destined for mine."

Happens at: Internet Exchange Points (IXPs)

Transit (Paid Service)

Small ISP → Pays Tier 1 ISP

"Route my traffic to the entire internet."

Cost: Based on bandwidth usage.

Internet Exchange Points (IXPs)

Physical locations where ISPs connect to exchange traffic.

Examples:

  • DE-CIX (Frankfurt) - World’s largest
  • AMS-IX (Amsterdam)
  • LINX (London)
  • JPNAP (Tokyo)

How It Works

ISP A ─┐
ISP B ─┼→ IXP Switch ←┬─ ISP C
ISP D ─┘                └─ ISP E

All ISPs connected to the switch can peer directly

Benefit: Lower latency, cheaper than transit.

Net Neutrality

Debate: Should ISPs treat all internet traffic equally?

With Net Neutrality

Netflix traffic → Full speed
Your blog traffic → Full speed
Competitor's video service → Full speed

All traffic treated equally.

Without Net Neutrality

Netflix (paid ISP) → Full speed
Your blog → Full speed
Competitor's video (didn't pay) → Throttled

ISPs can prioritize or throttle specific services.

The Big Takeaway

ISP in a Nutshell

An ISP (Internet Service Provider) connects you to the global internet.

What they provide:

  • Physical connectivity (cable, fiber, DSL, etc.)
  • Public IP address
  • DNS servers
  • Routing to the rest of the internet

ISP Tiers:

  • Tier 1: Global backbone (peer for free)
  • Tier 2: Regional (buy transit + peer)
  • Tier 3: Local (buy all transit)

Connection types:

  • Fiber (fastest, most reliable)
  • Cable (good, shared bandwidth)
  • DSL (ok, distance-dependent)
  • Satellite/5G (high latency, remote areas)

Privacy: ISPs can see unencrypted traffic. Use TLS (HTTPS) for privacy.

The rule: Your ISP is the gateway to the internet. They route your Packets and assign your public IP. Choose based on speed, reliability, and price. Always use HTTPS to prevent ISP snooping.
